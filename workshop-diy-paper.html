Abstract. We present a novel framework for classifying software defects in educational platforms, with specific application to Bug #42 in the Workshop-DIY ecosystem (N=39 applications, 7 domains). Through empirical analysis of 847 user sessions, 42 bug reports, and one formal court proceeding, we demonstrate that Bug #42 satisfies all criteria for feature classification under the proposed WKSHP-FC (Workshop Feature Classification) taxonomy. Our results show zero runtime errors (p < 0.001), a 23% increase in engagement metrics, and a successful legal defense. We conclude that the boundary between "bug" and "feature" is socially constructed and context-dependent, with implications for educational software development practices worldwide.

Keywords: Bug classification, feature engineering, educational technology, password123, coffee depletion, robot survivability

Introduction

The Workshop-DIY ecosystem comprises 39 browser-based educational applications spanning seven domains: robotics, artificial intelligence, electronics, 3D printing, programming, cybersecurity, and DevOps [1]. Since its founding in 2023, the platform has maintained a strict "zero installation" policy, requiring only a modern web browser for operation.

Bug #42 was first observed in the issue tracker on an indeterminate date by an unknown author. It appeared without a commit hash, pull request, or code review. Its initial description read: "I am not a bug. I am not a feature. I am the 40th" [2]. This paper examines whether Bug #42 meets the criteria for reclassification as a feature under our proposed taxonomy.

Discussion

Our findings suggest that the binary classification of software behavior as "bug" or "feature" is insufficient for educational contexts. Bug #42 exists in a liminal state â€” present but invisible, impactful but harmless, unauthorized but welcomed. We propose the term "emergent feature" for defects that satisfy WKSHP-FC criteria without deliberate implementation.

Limitations include the ongoing WiFi password situation (password123, N=3 rejected petitions), which may introduce confounding variables related to organizational decision-making patterns.
